Machine Directory

The Machine Directory contains a catalogue of some of the most influential pinball machines released throughout the years and a brief history of Pinball as a whole. The machines shown are ones we suggest and favoritize.

Origins During the Great Depression

Pinball, as we know it today, originated during the Great Depression, a time when affordable amusement was in high demand among Americans. It evolved from the French game bagatelle, where players guided balls past pins to score points by landing them in different holes on the playfield.

Early Development and Legal Challenges

The initial versions of pinball machines lacked flippers, making the game more about chance than skill as players manipulated the playfield to direct balls into scoring holes. Due to its luck-based gameplay, pinball was classified as gambling and was banned in the 1940s, despite the introduction of game-changing flippers in 1947.

- Humpty Dumpty is a pinball machine released by Gottlieb on October 25, 1947.[2] Named after Humpty Dumpty, the nursery rhyme character, it is the first pinball machine to include flippers

Technological Advancements in the 1950s

During the 1950s, companies like Williams, Bally, and Gottlieb led the industry in innovation, introducing features like multi-player capabilities, mechanical score reels, and enhanced playfield mechanisms. Despite these advancements, the machines remained electromechanical, relying on a complex array of moving parts.

The Shift to Electronic Gaming in the 1970s

In the 1960s and 1970s, pinball machines continued to evolve technologically but remained fundamentally electromechanical. The game was revolutionized in the late 1970s with the adoption of microprocessors, ushering in the era of electronic gaming with digital displays and complex gameplay features.

- Fireball is a historically notable pinball machine designed by Ted Zale and released by Bally in 1972. The table was one of the first to have a modern sci-fi/fantasy type of outer space theme and featured elaborate, painted artwork on the sides of the table.

- Playboy (Bally) is a licensed arcade pinball game released by Bally in 1978. Designed by Jim Patla with artwork by Paul Faris, it eventually sold over 18,000 units, making it one of the best-selling pinball tables ever.

- Gorgar is a 1979 pinball machine designed by Barry Oursler and released by Williams Electronics. It was the first speech-synthesized ("talking") pinball machine, containing a vocabulary of seven words!

Industry Challenges and the Rise of Video Games

By 1979, pinball reached its zenith with sales hitting 200,000 machines, but the early 1980s saw a drastic decline due to the popularity of arcade video games like Pac-Man and Space Invaders.

- Black Knight is a 1980 pinball machine designed by Steve Ritchie (who also provides the Knight's voice) and released by Williams Electronics. It was the first game with a two-level playfield and introduced magna-save!

- Haunted House is a pinball game released in October 31 1982 by Gottlieb. It was the first game with three playfields that the ball can move between, including one below the main playing surface.

- Pin-Bot (styled PIN•BOT) is a pinball machine released by Williams in October 1986. It was so successful, it was digitally ported to the NES and other videogame systems.

- Cyclone is a pinball machine released by Williams Electronics in 1988. It features an amusement park theme, Coney Island. Ronald Reagan and Nancy Reagan both appear in the backglass shown riding the rollercoaster.

Resurgence and Decline in the 1990s

- The early 1990s saw a resurgence in pinball popularity, highlighted by the success of The Addams Family game, which sold over 20,000 units. However, the mid-90s brought new challenges as home gaming consoles became prevalent, leading to a significant drop in pinball's appeal and the eventual closure of major manufacturers like Gottlieb and Williams.

- The Addams Family is a pinball machine released in March 1992. It was designed by Pat Lawlor and Larry DeMar and released by Midway (under the Bally label). It was based on the 1991 film of the same name, and features custom speech (mostly derived from the motion picture) by the stars of the film, Anjelica Huston and Raul Julia.

- Creature from the Black Lagoon is a pinball machine designed by John Trudeau ("Dr. Flash") and released by Midway (under the Bally brand name). It is loosely based on the movie of the same name. The game's theme is 1950s drive-in theater. It released December of 1992.

- Twilight Zone is a widebody pinball machine, designed by Pat Lawlor and based on the TV series of the same name. It was first released in 1993 by Midway (under the Bally label).

- Attack from Mars is a 1995 pinball game designed by Brian Eddy, and released by Midway (under the Bally label). It is considered a very accessible game for all audiences!

- Medieval Madness is a Williams pinball machine released in June 1997. Designed by Brian Eddy and programmed by Lyman Sheats, it had a production run of 4,016 units.

The Modern Era and Revival

The closure of Williams' pinball division in 1999 left Stern Pinball as the sole major manufacturer until Jersey Jack Pinball entered the scene in 2011, sparking innovation and competition. Today, companies like Stern, Jersey Jack, and others are developing sophisticated machines, blending modern technology with classic gameplay, and helping to lead a pinball revival.

- Spider-Man is a pinball machine designed by Steve Ritchie and manufactured by Stern Pinball that was first released in June 2007. The table encompasses all three films in Sam Raimi's Spider-Man trilogy, based on the comics.

- Ghostbusters is a pinball machine designed by John Trudeau and released by Stern Pinball in June 2016. It is based on the Ghostbusters films of the same name. Ernie Hudson provided custom speech and guides the player into becoming the latest member of the Ghostbusters team.

- Godzilla (ゴジラ Gojira) is a pinball machine manufactured by Stern Pinball in collaboration with Toho International. It was released around the world beginning in 2021. Based on the Godzilla films of the Showa era, the Godzilla pinball machine features numerous monsters from the era along with authentic video and audio from ten Showa Godzilla films. It also features the song "Godzilla" by Blue Öyster Cult as its title music.

Pinball's Cultural Comeback

Today, pinball is experiencing a cultural resurgence, becoming increasingly popular in museum arcades and among new generations of players. Notably, the hobby of pinball received a massive resurgence during the Covid-19 epidemic, as many people were stuck in their homes; prices sored for a few years but have since come back down to normal market value! Pinball still remains a beloved pastime that is celebrated for its social, addictive, and entertaining qualities, securing its place in American culture.
